What Are Urban Heat Islands? (UHIs)

An Urban Heat Island, or UHI, is a metropolitan area that's a lot warmer than the rural areas surrounding it.

Urban Heat Islands (UHIs) are areas within cities that become significantly warmer than surrounding rural areas. This temperature difference—often 15°F to 20°F higher—is caused by the way cities are built and the amount of human activity they contain.

In densely developed urban areas, buildings, roads, and infrastructure absorb and retain heat. Unlike rural areas, cities lack trees and green spaces that provide shade and cooling. Combined with vehicle emissions and energy use, these factors create hot zones that can be uncomfortable and dangerous during heatwaves.

Exploring Urban Heat Islands Through Satellite Imagery

This interactive site explores urban heat islands (UHIs) in three U.S. cities: Atlanta, GA, Washington, D.C., and Boston, MA. Using Landsat imagery from two time periods for each city, we compare land surface temperature (LST) to reveal how heat distribution has changed.

Satellite images were downloaded from USGS EarthExplorer. This data helps us visualize UHI growth linked to urban expansion, infrastructure, and climate.

Click a city in the menu to explore its interactive LST map. You can use the layer toggle tool to compare older and newer maps. Scroll down on each city’s page for an explanation of the temperature map and a reference image of the city.

These visual tools aim to spark conversation about sustainable city design and the importance of green infrastructure in reducing heat buildup.
